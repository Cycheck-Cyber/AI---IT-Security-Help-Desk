The Problem

European organisations are accountable for every byte of personal data they send abroad — yet almost none can describe, in real time, where that data actually flows.

🌫️
Outbound traffic is a blind spot
Every browser, SaaS app, smart device, and background service phones home to dozens of destinations. Firewalls log, but nobody reads. Cloud dashboards show what runs inside; nothing shows what runs out.
🌍
Location ≠ Sovereignty
A data centre in Frankfurt operated by a US company is still subject to US law. Under the CLOUD Act, US authorities can compel that operator to hand over your data — without a German court order, without notice. The pin on the map is misleading.
📋
Annual reviews can't keep up
Procurement reviews and DPIAs happen once. Your network changes every minute. A new SaaS tool, a software update, a third-party SDK — each can silently route personal data through a new jurisdiction. The spreadsheet is wrong by lunchtime.
The Legal Reality

Four legal instruments define what EU data sovereignty actually means today — and why "hosted in Europe" is no longer a sufficient answer.

2018 · EU
GDPR
Articles 44–49
Personal data may only leave the EU under specific safeguards: an adequacy decision, Standard Contractual Clauses, or a derogation. Every transfer must be documented and justified.
2018 · USA
CLOUD Act
Compels US-incorporated companies to disclose data they hold or control — regardless of where the servers are located. Applies to every US cloud and SaaS provider, including their European subsidiaries.
2008 · USA
FISA Section 702
Authorises bulk surveillance of non-US persons by US intelligence agencies through US "electronic communication service providers". Operates without individual warrants and without notice to the data subject.
2020 · CJEU
Schrems II
The European Court of Justice ruled that EU contractual clauses do not, on their own, neutralise US surveillance law. Controllers must verify, case by case, that equivalent protection exists — or stop the transfer.
